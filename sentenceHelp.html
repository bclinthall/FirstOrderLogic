Sentence Help

  1. The most basic sort of sentence is a literal.

    A literal is a single capital letter that represents a sentence.

    You might, for example, use E to symbolize "John bought eggs".

    Literals are simple sentences.

  2. Complex sentences are sentences that have other sentences as parts.

    Complex sentences are formed by attaching operators to sentences

  3. Conjunction: The conjunction operator is symbolized by the & symbol.

    If p is a sentence (simple or complex) and q is a sentence (simple or complex) then p&q is a sentence.

    p&q means p and q.

  4. Disjunction: The disjunction operator is symbolized by the | symbol.

    If p is a sentence (simple or complex) and q is a sentence (simple or complex) then p|q is a sentence.

    p|q means p or q.

  5. Conditional: The conditional operator is symbolized by the > symbol.

    If p is a sentence (simple or complex) and q is a sentence (simple or complex) then p>q is a sentence.

    p>q means if p then q.

  6. Negation: The negation operator is symbolized by the ~ symbol.

    If p is a sentence (simple or complex) then ~p is a sentence.

    ~p means it is not the case that p.

  7. For our purposes it is also helpful to have one other symbol. Our contradiction symbol will be *.

    Like a literal, the contradiction symbol is a simple sentence. It may serve as any part of any complex sentence, just like a literal.

  8. Parentheses

    A conjunction, disjunction, or conditional may be enclosed in parentheses. When it is a subsentence, it must be enclosed in parentheses. This is the only proper use of parentheses.

    So, these are grammatical sentences: A&B, (A&B), A&(B|C)

    These are not grammatical sentences: (A), (~A), (A)&B, A&B&C, A|B&C

These are the rules for forming grammatical sentences. Here are some examples of ungrammatical sentences: B&, |A, B&&C, A~B, ~AB, >
